Interaction design for a new Biodiversity Net Gain Register, advancing nature's recovery by supporting housing development and increasing habitats across England by 10%.

The challenge

The Environment Act (2021) requires developers to deliver a 'biodiversity net gain' as a condition of their planning permission. To deliver biodiversity gains, habitats can be enhanced on the developers own building site, or at an off-site location, by paying a landowner to manage their land for nature recovery.

The problem we tried to solve; How do we collect data about off-site biodiversity gains and enable Government and local planning authorities to check that they are legitimate, accountable, and transparent?

What I did

Working in a multi-discplinary team, I used agile and design thinking methodolgy to help understand and solve problems.

Identified riskiest assumptions

To identify our riskiest assumptions and give the team focus, I helped facilitate an assumption mapping workshop, involving people from the business, policy and other key stakeholders.

Mapped out 'as-is' journeys

To understand how biodiversity net gain was currently working, I mapped out our users current journey.

To create the map, I first facilitated workshops involving the business, policy and subject matter experts.

Working closely with a user researcher and content designer, I validated and iterated the map with several rounds of user research. This was a great way to start understanding our users and their needs.

Team brainstorm

To create a shared understanding of a problem and generate lots of ideas, I facilitated design workshops, often involving the whole team so that we had lots of different perspectives on solving the problem.

Learning by prototyping

To experiment and test ideas with users, I built various HTML prototypes using the GOV.UK Prototype Kit. Using the prototypes as stimulus to create conversations with our users, resulting in an increased understanding of their needs.

After each round of research, I iterated designs and tried different solutions based on user findings. I kept track of all our design hypotheses and findings in a design decisions log. This proved extremely useful when preparing for our GDS assessment.

Result

In March 2022, I represented design for our service at our GDS Alpha service assessment. A panel of experienced specialists from the government digital community assessed the service and agreed that we met the GDS Service Standard and so we passed our Alpha assessment.
